The NERVE Architecture: How We Built Autonomous AI Outreach That Doesn't Spam

By Bit Rage Labour • March 1, 2026 • 14 min read

NERVE — the Networked Engine for Revenue and Venture Execution — is the daemon that runs Bit Rage Labour's autonomous sales pipeline. It prospects, qualifies, writes personalized outreach, schedules follow-ups, monitors inbox responses, and manages multi-touch sequences across channels. All without a human in the loop.

This is how we built an AI outreach system that achieves 34% open rates and 12% reply rates — numbers that beat most human-run SDR operations — while maintaining strict anti-spam compliance.

What NERVE Actually Does

NERVE is a background daemon (automation/nerve.py) that runs continuously on our Railway deployment. Every 15 minutes, it executes a cycle:

1. Prospect Discovery

Scans configured sources for new leads matching our ICP (Ideal Customer Profile). Sources include job board postings, LinkedIn activity signals, company news, and inbound website visitors.

2. Qualification

Scores each prospect against 12 criteria: company size, industry, tech stack, hiring signals, funding stage, content activity, pain indicators, budget signals, decision-maker access, geographic fit, competitive usage, and timing signals.

3. Enrichment

Gathers additional context: recent company news, social posts, job listings, tech stack changes. This context feeds personalization.

4. Sequence Generation

The Sales Ops agent writes a 3-email sequence personalized to each prospect. Every email references a real signal — a job posting, a product launch, a funding round. Never generic, never templated.

5. Send & Schedule

Sends the first email, schedules follow-ups at 3-day and 7-day intervals. Respects sending windows (9am-5pm in the recipient's timezone) and daily volume limits.

6. Inbox Monitor

Reads responses, classifies them (interested, meeting request, not interested, out of office, unsubscribe), and takes appropriate action. Interested replies get flagged for human follow-up. Unsubscribes are immediately honored.

Signal-Based Prospecting

The biggest difference between NERVE and every other outreach tool is that we refuse to do spray-and-pray. Every email must reference a real signal — a verifiable reason why the prospect would benefit from our services right now.

Our prospect engine (automation/prospect_engine.py) identifies signals like:

  • Hiring signals: Company posted "Looking for a Virtual Assistant" or "Hiring Content Writer" → they have the need, we have the solution at lower cost and faster turnaround.
  • Pain signals: Support ticket volume spikes (public review sites), customer complaints about response times, job postings for roles we automate.
  • Growth signals: New funding round, product launch, geographic expansion → they'll need content, outreach, and support at scale.
  • Tech signals: Company using Zapier/Make for automation → they're already automation-minded, we're a more powerful alternative.
The rule: If the prospect engine can't find a real signal, the prospect doesn't get contacted. Period. We'd rather send 10 perfectly targeted emails than 1,000 generic ones.

The Email Architecture

Every outreach email follows a strict structure enforced by the QA pipeline:

Email 1: The Signal-Led Opener

Subject: [Specific signal reference] — under 60 chars
Line 1: Reference the signal ("Saw you're hiring a VA for...")
Line 2: Bridge to value ("We handle that with AI agents...")
Line 3: Social proof ("We process X tasks/month for Y clients...")
Line 4: Soft CTA ("Worth a 10-min call this week?")

Email 2: The Value Add (Day 3)

Subject: Re: [original subject]
Line 1: Reference Email 1 without being pushy
Line 2: Add a new piece of value (case study, metric, insight)
Line 3: Lower the commitment ("Happy to send a sample output")

Email 3: The Breakup (Day 7)

Subject: Re: [original subject]  
Line 1: Acknowledge they're busy
Line 2: One-sentence value reminder
Line 3: Permission-based close ("Should I check back in a month?")

The QA agent validates every email against these rules before it's queued for sending. If any rule is violated — subject too long, no signal reference, aggressive language — the email is regenerated.

Anti-Spam: Not Optional

We take anti-spam extremely seriously. Not because we're noble — because getting our domain blacklisted would destroy the business. Here's how we stay clean:

Technical Controls

  • SPF, DKIM, DMARC configured on our domain (verified via automation/bimi_setup.py)
  • Volume limits: Maximum 50 new outreach emails per day. This is a hard cap enforced in code, not a guideline.
  • Sending windows: Only between 9am-5pm in the recipient's timezone. No weekend sends.
  • Warmup protocol: New sending domains start at 5 emails/day, increasing by 5 per week until reaching the 50 cap.
  • Bounce handling: Hard bounces immediately remove the address. 2+ soft bounces within 7 days = removal.

Content Controls

  • Banned phrases (config/banned_phrases.txt): We maintain a list of 200+ spam trigger words and phrases. The QA agent checks every email against this list.
  • No attachments: Ever. Attachments trigger spam filters and look suspicious from unknown senders.
  • Plain text fallback: Every HTML email has a plain text version. Some enterprise mail servers strip HTML.
  • Unsubscribe link: Every email includes a one-click unsubscribe. It's not just CAN-SPAM compliance — it's Google's bulk sender requirements since February 2024.

Behavioral Controls

  • Reply detection: If someone replies (even negatively), the sequence stops immediately. No automated follow-ups after any human response.
  • Deduplication: NERVE tracks every contact across all sequences. No person gets contacted twice, even if they appear in multiple prospect lists.
  • Cool-off periods: After a 3-email sequence completes with no response, the contact enters a 90-day cool-off. After that, they're eligible for recontact only if a new signal appears.

The Follow-Up Engine

Follow-up scheduling is managed by a JSON-based state machine (automation/followups.json). Each active sequence has:

{
  "prospect_id": "abc123",
  "email": "prospect@company.com",
  "sequence_step": 1,
  "next_send_at": "2026-03-02T14:00:00Z",
  "signal": "hiring-va-posting-feb-28",
  "emails_sent": [
    {
      "step": 1,
      "sent_at": "2026-02-28T10:30:00Z",
      "subject": "Your VA posting on Indeed",
      "provider": "zoho",
      "status": "delivered"
    }
  ],
  "status": "active",
  "replies": []
}

Every NERVE cycle scans this file for sequences where next_send_at has passed and status is still active. It generates the next email in the sequence, validates it through QA, and sends it.

Why JSON Instead of a Database?

We knew from the start that our follow-up state would be small — at 50 emails/day, we have at most ~150 active sequences at any time. JSON is human-readable, easy to debug, and trivially backed up. We snapshot it daily (see followups_backup_*.json). If we scale past 500 active sequences, we'll migrate to SQLite. Not before.

Inbox Reading: Closing the Loop

The most technically interesting part of NERVE is the inbox reader (automation/inbox_reader.py). It connects to our Zoho email via IMAP, reads new messages, and uses AI classification to determine intent:

# Response classification categories
CLASSIFICATIONS = {
    "interested":    "Prospect shows buying interest",
    "meeting":       "Prospect wants to schedule a call",
    "question":      "Prospect has questions about services",
    "not_interested": "Polite decline or not a fit",
    "out_of_office":  "Auto-reply, vacation, OOO",
    "unsubscribe":    "Wants to stop receiving emails",
    "bounce":         "Delivery failure notification",
    "spam_report":    "Marked as spam (CRITICAL)",
}

Each classification triggers a different action:

  • interested / meeting / question → Stop the sequence, log to inbox_log.json, send a Slack/email notification for immediate human follow-up
  • not_interested → Stop the sequence, mark contact as declined, respect their decision permanently
  • out_of_office → Pause the sequence, reschedule follow-up for the return date mentioned in the OOO reply
  • unsubscribe → Immediately add to suppression list, stop all sequences, confirm unsubscribe
  • bounce → Remove from mailing list, check if domain-wide (MX failure) or address-specific
  • spam_report → Emergency alert. Full sequence audit. If we get 2 spam reports from the same campaign, that campaign is suspended for manual review.

The Decision Log

Everything NERVE does is logged in the decision log (automation/decision_log.py). Every prospect found, every email sent, every reply received, every classification made. The log serves three purposes:

  1. Auditability: We can trace any outreach interaction from discovery to resolution.
  2. Optimization: We analyze which signals produce the highest reply rates, which email structures get the best engagement, which industries convert best.
  3. Compliance: If someone asks "why did you email me?", we can show exactly which signal triggered the outreach and that we followed all anti-spam rules.

Orchestration: Bringing It All Together

NERVE doesn't run in isolation. The orchestrator (automation/orchestrator.py) coordinates NERVE with the rest of the system:

Orchestrator ├── NERVE (outreach daemon) │ ├── Prospect Engine │ ├── Sales Ops Agent │ ├── Email Sender │ └── Inbox Reader ├── Revenue Daemon │ ├── Pipeline tracking │ ├── Invoice generation │ └── Payment follow-up └── Self-Check ├── System health ├── API status └── LLM provider health

The orchestrator ensures that NERVE doesn't conflict with manual operations. If a user manually sends an email to a prospect via the dashboard, NERVE detects it and pauses the automated sequence. If a client task comes in for a company that NERVE is prospecting, the prospect engine marks that company as "converted" and stops outreach.

What's Next for NERVE

We're building three additions:

  1. Multi-channel sequences: Adding LinkedIn connection requests and Twitter/X DMs as secondary touchpoints. Email stays primary, but some prospects engage more on social.
  2. A/B testing: Automatically split-test subject lines, CTAs, and email structures. Currently we optimize manually by reviewing engagement data weekly.
  3. Conversation AI: For "question" replies, generate a helpful AI response for human review. The human still sends it, but the draft is ready instantly.
